Heterogeneous Treatment Effects of Intensive Blood Pressure Control on Major Adverse Cardiovascular Events in Nondiabetic Patients Manuscript
Socioeconomically disadvantaged populations face a higher risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD), a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in the United States. Thus, it is important to address these risks effectively to reduce health disparities. While blood pressure (BP) control is a crucial strategy to reduce CVD risk, the uniformity of treatment effects from intensive BP control and BP-lowering medications across different patient subpopulations—each with unique medical histories, biomarkers, and socioeconomic statuses—remains unclear. Hypothesizing these treatment effects may vary across subpopulations, we aim to seek the subgroup with the most benefit by identifying key modifiers contributing to this heterogeneity. Using data from the Systolic blood PRessure INtervention Trial, we focused on the time to the first major adverse cardiovascular events as our outcome, which includes CVD death, fatal or non-fatal myocardial infarction, non-MI acute coronary syndrome, fatal or non-fatal stroke, and fatal or non-fatal heart failure. The intervention of interest is an intensive BP control strategy targeting a systolic BP of less than 120 mmHg. We employed advanced machine-learning techniques such as causal survival forests for model training and Shapley Additive exPlanations values for interpretation, where the identified key modifiers include biomarkers related to metabolic, lipid metabolic, renal, electrolyte, and cardiovascular function, as well as cognitive assessment measures, and general health and physical measurements. Finally, we found the heterogeneities in hazard ratios across subgroups defined by these key modifiers, using the Cox proportional hazards model.

Keywords: Cardiovascular disease, blood pressure control, Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial (SPRINT), heterogeneous treatment effects, causal survival forests, Cox proportional hazards model, health disparities.



1. Identifying Novel Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) Subgroups among the General US Population: A Machine Learning Approach Poster   Manuscript
An increasing emphasis has been placed on addressing social determinants of health (SDOH) through clinical and policy-level interventions. However, SDOH encompasses a wide range of intercorrelated social constructs, and these needs often have deeper roots and correlations with one another. This complexity poses significant challenges in designing effective and targeted screening and intervention strategies. This study aims to leverage advanced machine learning algorithms to cluster the US population into subgroups with distinctive SDOH characteristics, providing valuable insights into the intricate landscape of SDOH needs. Survey data on 266 individual-level SDOH variables, linked with electronic health records from 28,778 participants of the All of Us research program, was utilized. A stepwise clustering approach was employed, using the partitioning around medoids (PAM) alg orithm with the Calinski-Harabasz (CH) index for initial feature selection. PAM was then reapplied to the selected key features to determine the final clusters. Each cluster was labeled based on its distinctive characteristics, and we examined whether these SDOH clusters exhibited unique disease profiles, including the prevalence of type 2 diabetes, obesity, and a history of pneumonia. Clustering pre-retirees (45-64) and elderly (65+) participants revealed five and eight distinct SDOH groups, respectively. Several key variables were common among both pre-retirees and the elderly, including labor market activity, affordability of medical care, and smoking behavior. For pre-retirees, additional factors strongly influenced clustering into different SDOH groups in cluded annual household income, highest degree in education, neighborhood safety and their income, negative experience in healthcare and healthcare coverage, ethnicity, marital status, sexual orientation, presence of supportive relationships, and religiousness. For the elderly, neighbors' attentiveness to home maintenance, frequency of relocation, healthcare utilization rates, cultural preferences for healthcare providers, insurance coverage, and experiences of disrespect emerged as pivotal variables, marking a noteworthy shift in SDOH from pre-retiree to retired. Clusters characterized by higher SDOH statuses exhibited a significantly lower prevalence of type 2 diabetes, current obesity, and a history of pneumonia. Findings can inform the development of targeted SDOH screening and interventions.

2. Specification and Estimation of Spatial Interaction Models for Nonnegative Origin-destination Flows Manuscript
The gravity equation has a long-standing empirical tradition in estimating international trade flows. Despite its widespread use, the traditional gravity model for Origin-Destination (OD) flows has significant limitations. First, it accounts only for bilateral interactions, lacking a broader multilateral context that includes multilateral resistance and third-party effects. Second, the log-transformed specification for outcomes may lead to invalid inferences. Therefore, we advocate for the use of outcome data in its original level form, while incorporating multilateral resistance and spillover effects. We propose a novel spatial interaction model for nonnegative OD flows, integrating spatial weighting matrices and additive fixed effects into the Poisson maximum likelihood estimation. Additionally, we adjust standard errors to ensure robustness against heteroscedasticity and correlated errors. Monte Carlo simulations demonstrate favorable finite sample properties of our estimator. Finally, an empirical application using trade data provides strong evidence of spillover effects resulting from multilateral interactions based on geographic, economic, and political proximities.

Keywords: Origin-destination (OD) flows, gravity equation, third-party effects, log transformation, multilateral resistance, Poisson pseudo maximum likelihood estimation.
